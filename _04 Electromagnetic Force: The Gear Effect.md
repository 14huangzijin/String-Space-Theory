

In the last chapter, we explained gravity, one of the four fundamental forces. Now let us examine the electromagnetic force.

What is the electromagnetic force?

Simply put: "like repels like, opposites attract."

"Like" refers to the same type of electric charge. "Opposite" refers to different types of electric charge.

All electrons carry a negative charge, so they repel each other.

Electrons carry a negative charge. Protons in the atomic nucleus carry a positive charge, so they attract each other.

We have already created the electron's structure by twisting a rope: it is a counter-clockwise twisted topological single loop with a charge of −1.

The proton's structure is more complex, and we will discuss it later. But its total charge is +1.

The positron, which exists in symmetry with the electron, is twisted clockwise and carries a charge of +1.

So, what fundamentally determines positive or negative charge?

It is the twist direction of the topological loop.

The electron is twisted counter-clockwise, so it carries a negative charge. The positron is twisted clockwise, so it carries a positive charge.

Then what is the mechanism behind "like repels like, opposites attract"?

The answer is gears.

Imagine two rotating gears. If they rotate in the same direction (say, both clockwise), then when they approach each other, the direction of motion at their contact point is opposite—one moving up, the other down.

This causes them to collide and bounce apart. This is "like repels like."

But if two gears rotate in opposite directions, when they draw near, you will find that the direction of motion at their contact point is exactly the same.

This allows their teeth and grooves to perfectly interlock and clamp tight. Trying to pull them apart again is extremely difficult. This force that bites down and holds the other is "opposites attract."

The principle of the electromagnetic force follows the same logic as this gear analogy.

**The Source of the Electromagnetic Force**

In standard physics, how a particle can continuously emit electromagnetic force without needing to "recharge" is a mystery. But in String-Space Theory, the answer is clear: the energy for the electromagnetic force comes from the string-web's background tremor.

The entire string-web, bearing the vibrations of countless energy events, is perpetually in an extremely low-frequency, omnipresent faint quivering. This background tremor is the energy source for all electromagnetic forces in the universe.

When this trembling energy propagates along a string and enters a material loop (such as an electron), something remarkable happens. The loop acts like a converter, transforming this irregular, faint quivering into a circular thrust, which is then radiated outward in the form of a spiral wave. This outward-radiating spiral wave is the electromagnetic force.

A topological loop has a twist direction, and its twist direction determines whether the spiral wave it radiates is clockwise or counter-clockwise.

The electron's structure is a counter-clockwise twisted loop, so the spiral wave it emits rotates counter-clockwise.

The positron is twisted clockwise, so the spiral wave it emits naturally rotates clockwise.

But an important note: the twist direction is not determined by whether the string within the loop you see is a clockwise or counter-clockwise circle. The twist direction is not the loop's direction.

The only intuitive way to check is to pull two loops together. If they cancel each other out, they have opposite directions. If they do not cancel, they have the same direction.

Twist-force is the twist direction of the string inside the loop, not the direction of the loop itself.

A screw has its thread direction fixed at the moment of manufacture. Similarly, when a segment of string is twisted into a loop, that twist is solidified within its geometric structure.

The electron and the positron are two loops with completely opposite twist directions, like a left-handed screw and a right-handed screw. With different twist directions, the gear directions of the spiral waves they radiate are naturally different.

Like a screw, it spirals as it turns. The twist direction determines whether it is a clockwise or counter-clockwise gear.

Like repels like. Opposites attract.

This is the electromagnetic force.

Although the essence of the electromagnetic force is the gear effect, in a single instant, the spiral wave of one loop only acts upon a single plane.

If this plane were fixed, then the electromagnetic force would only be effective in a two-dimensional plane, not the force we experience in reality that acts in all directions.

But in fact, the material loop is not static. It is constantly changing its position and bulging direction within the string-web's background tremor. Billions of random jumps per second cause the plane of its radiated spiral wave to flip at high speed. In an extremely short time, this gear effect sweeps across all directions.

At the same time, the other loop interacting with it is undergoing the same high-speed flipping.

Two randomly flipping gears, viewed on a macroscopic timescale, have their interaction averaged across all directions.

Thus, a force that is essentially only effective on a two-dimensional plane manifests as isotropy in three-dimensional space.

The spherical symmetry of the electromagnetic force is, just like gravity, a statistical result of the loop's high-speed jumping across the string-web.

**From Magnets to Magnetic Fields**

Now, let us move from individual particles to the macroscopic world to explain magnetic fields.

If you have a large basin filled with water and stir it clockwise with one hand, you will see a vortex.

Now, put both hands in the water and stir them clockwise simultaneously, tracing two separate circles. You will notice that outside these two small vortices, a larger vortex encompassing both of them forms.

This is the principle of magnetic fields. Countless spiral waves of the same direction, although they repel each other, will superimpose their spirals to form a more powerful macroscopic spiral wave—provided their rotation axes are aligned.

However, there is a prerequisite for the superposition of spirals: their "rotation axes" must be aligned. Two vortices both rotating clockwise on the water surface have parallel rotation axes and can superimpose perfectly.

But if your left hand stirs clockwise on the water surface while your right hand stirs clockwise perpendicular to the water surface, they cannot form a unified large vortex.

An ordinary piece of iron has countless electron loops inside it. Although they all carry the same charge, the directions of their individual rotation axes are chaotic. Macroscopically, the spiral waves they produce cancel each other out, becoming a noise with no discernible melody.

Magnetization is the process of applying a strong external magnetic field to forcibly reorient all the electron loops' rotation axes in the same direction. When the rotation axes of these "gears" are aligned, the countless tiny spiral waves they emit instantly superimpose in the same direction, forming a powerful, macroscopic spiral-wave flow field.

This unified force is the magnetic force displayed by a magnet.

Thus, the magnetic field lines we see—emerging from the magnet's north pole, looping around to point toward its south pole—are nothing more than a vast vortex composed of these countless tiny vortices.

Energy emerges from the north pole, loops through external space back to the south pole, and then flows internally from south back to north, completing a spiral cycle. This is the magnetic field.
