## Energy That Never Dissipates

A single string has only two modes of motion and one type of positional relationship. Strings relate to each other only through perpendicular intersections. Under normal circumstances, energy does not dissipate.

When light (a symmetric wave) and spiral pulses run along a string, they pass through countless perpendicular intersection points. At each intersection point, the light wave slaps against the perpendicular string, pushing it upward.

When the perpendicular string is pushed up, it simultaneously stores two kinds of potential energy: one is elastic potential energy—having been pushed away from its original straight position, the string's elasticity tries to pull it back; the other is torsional potential energy—the light wave's slap is not perfectly symmetrical and imparts a tiny twist to the perpendicular string.

After the light wave's crest passes, the wave begins its downhill descent. At this moment, the two kinds of potential energy stored in the perpendicular string are released along with the downhill force, feeding back into the wave on the original string.

Pushing up and pressing back are different phases of the same wave. During the push-up, energy transfers from the wave to the perpendicular string. During the press-back, energy transfers from the perpendicular string back to the wave. A complete peak-trough cycle perfectly completes one full loan and repayment. What is borrowed is exactly what is repaid. The net loss is zero.

This is why light can travel through the universe for tens of billions of years without attenuation. Perpendicular intersection points are not energy leaks; they are temporary energy storage and return points. The string-web's topological structure inherently prevents energy from diffusing into a third string—the small segment of the perpendicular string that is excited upward has its elastic potential energy acting solely upon the wave-bearing string itself. With no third party to receive the energy, it must be returned to the wave-bearing string.

**High-Energy Light Waves Transform into Spiral Pulses**

However, when energy reaches a certain extreme, high-energy light waves will transform into spiral pulses.

We can use an analogy to understand the process of high-energy light transforming into matter: a motorcycle and a mountain peak.

If a motorcycle traverses a gentle slope, it rides up from the base and then rides down from the peak back to the base. During this process, the number of times its wheels turn on the way up is exactly equal to the number of times they turn on the way down.

But if the motorcycle leaps over a mountain of extreme amplitude, upon flying to the peak, it does not immediately descend. Instead, it hangs in the air for a period of time. During this hang time, its wheels continue to spin. Energy is lost to the air and not fully returned to the mountain slope.

Now, imagine the perpendicular string as a stationary motorcycle, and the light wave as a continuous mountain range rushing toward it. The motorcycle itself does not move. It is the mountain that runs. The spinning of the wheels is the twist generated in the perpendicular string as the wave surface pushes it upward.

When the light wave has low frequency and small amplitude, the mountain peak is gentle. The motorcycle is slowly pushed up, its wheels turning slowly. For every turn made going up, an equal and opposite turn is made coming down. The wheels' rotation is completely returned to the mountain. The motorcycle lands steadily back in place, having taken nothing away. This is the scenario when low-energy light passes a perpendicular intersection point—energy borrowed is perfectly repaid in full.

When the light wave has extremely high frequency and massive amplitude, the mountain peak becomes extremely steep. The motorcycle is violently launched into the sky, flying over the peak, hanging in the air. During the hang time, the mountain has already passed beneath. With no mountain under the wheels, the rotation cannot be returned to it. The wheels' rotation can only act upon the motorcycle itself—this is the perpendicular string being twisted.

That is to say, when a high-energy light wave acts upon a perpendicular string, the perpendicular string is pushed upward, and during the hang time, its own twist-force is not transferred back to the light wave but instead twists upon itself. This process is the high-energy light wave losing a portion of its energy, which is transformed into a spiral pulse on the perpendicular string.

If the light wave is a continuous wave train of high frequency and high amplitude, each wave crest is a hang time event. During each hang time, the wheels spin freely for a moment, causing the perpendicular string to twist upon itself. Twist accumulates turn by turn, and the spiral pulse ultimately transforms into a topological loop—that is, matter.

This is the process of high-energy light transforming into matter—it first causes the perpendicular string to acquire a twist, and this twist is ultimately locked into a topological structure, becoming matter.

Why does low-frequency light, even with large amplitude, not transform into matter? Because as long as the mountain peak is gentle enough, the motorcycle stays glued to the mountain surface and never experiences hang time. Without hang time, every turn of the wheels is returned on the way down. No matter how tall the mountain, as long as it is gentle enough, the motorcycle lands safely.

This explains why matter transformation has a frequency threshold rather than an amplitude threshold. The threshold is not determined by total energy, but by the ratio between the steepness of the wave crest and the response time of the perpendicular string. The elastic constant k sets the response time of the perpendicular string, and thus sets the threshold for the birth of matter.
