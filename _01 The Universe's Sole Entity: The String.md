

General Relativity tells us that time, space, matter, and energy are four things that can influence and transform into one another. This implies that if the universe has a single underlying entity, then these four are merely different states in which that entity manifests.

That underlying entity cannot be a fundamental particle. A particle is too small—it cannot contain space.

So let us begin to imagine. Imagine that space can be dense or sparse, like a wad of cotton that can be compressed and stretched. Now, picture that cotton as countless strings. Where space is dense, the strings are packed close together, their density high. Where space is sparse, they lie far apart, their density low.

That's right—the sole entity is an infinite number of infinitely long strings. This is not the mysterious concept from Superstring Theory, vibrating in ten-dimensional spacetime. These are real strings that exist in our three-dimensional space, spanning the cosmos, infinite in length.

The strings are infinite in length and infinitely thin in diameter. In their natural state, they are arranged perpendicular or parallel to one another, packed extremely tightly, weaving together into a three-dimensional, infinite string-web that fills the entire universe.

But strings alone are not enough. The strings must also possess elasticity. Because without elasticity, the very idea of "dense or sparse arrangement" cannot exist. Without elasticity, the string-web cannot be compressed or stretched, and no change can ever occur.

So the strings must be elastic. Picture the space around a black hole—extremely dense. This is, in reality, the string-web being compressed tightly by the black hole's gravity.

If we now magically remove the black hole, the compressed strings, driven by their own elasticity, will rapidly expand outward, releasing the stored elastic potential energy.

And what if the black hole is not magically removed, but explodes for some reason? It would instantly release an immense amount of energy and matter. At the same time, the extremely compressed, dense space around it would violently spring outward, releasing even more energy and matter in the process of dilution.

This is the Big Bang.

Indeed, the "Singularity Big Bang" of our universe was the explosion of a "Mother Black Hole"—a black hole of staggering mass, equivalent to the total mass and energy of our entire universe.

The explosion was a process of releasing the string-web, stretched to its breaking point, outward along with matter and energy. Our universe is not a bubble born from "nothing." It is a process of extremely compressed space regressing from density toward sparsity.

In other words, our universe is not "expanding"—space is "diluting."

Outside this diluting "universe bubble," space still exists. For now, let us call that space the "Greater Universe." The string-web density in the Greater Universe is lower. Because the density of our universe's Mother Black Hole was so high, after its explosion, these compact strings have been in a continuous process of outward dilution.

We will discuss the details of the diluting universe later.

Now, let us return to the most fundamental entity of this cosmos—the string.

The string is the sole entity of the universe. Time, space, matter, and energy are nothing more than different states of the string. The string is infinitely long and has a natural tendency to remain straight.

Why does the string tend to remain straight? Because a straight line is its lowest energy state. Like a piece of springy metal wire, you can force it to bend, but the moment you let go, it snaps back to being straight.

Returning to straight means returning to the lowest and most stable energy state. When a string is bent, it stores elastic potential energy. The more it bends, the greater the stored energy.

And stored energy must eventually be released. A black hole compresses space—meaning it compresses the string-web. This compressed string-web stores an enormous amount of elastic potential energy. Ultimately, it all ends with "the elastic dilution of the strings."

Thus, we arrive at the most fundamental postulate of the entire theory: the universe has a sole entity—the string.

The string is infinite in length. In its natural state, strings are arranged perpendicular or parallel to one another, forming an infinite String-Space. The string possesses elasticity, and this very elasticity is the elastic tension generated when a bent string tries to pull itself straight again.

And the Big Bang is the grand process of dilution, from dense to sparse, as the string-web, compressed to its limit by the Mother Black Hole, finally springs open after the explosion.
